Long before kickoff, a night game is already a balance sheet. The floodlights alone cost more to run than most of the players earn in a week. And yet, every season, the turnstiles click.

## The cost of light

Switching on a full bank of floodlights is the single most expensive decision a small club makes on matchday. It is also non-negotiable — the night game *is* the product. People do not come for football in the abstract. They come for football against the dark.

## Who actually pays

- The club pays for light, stewarding, and grass it can barely afford.
- The fan pays for ninety minutes and a reason to leave the house.
- The town, quietly, pays for nothing and gets the most: an evening where everyone is in the same place at the same time.

> The crowd is not buying a result. It is buying the *possibility* of one — and possibility, unlike a trophy, is in stock every single week.

## The real return

The accountants will tell you these fixtures barely break even. They are measuring the wrong thing. The return on a night game is not printed on a ticket. It is the walk home afterwards, arguing about a refereeing decision that, by morning, nobody will remember — and that everyone will somehow still be talking about.
