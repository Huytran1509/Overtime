We talk about the 100m as if it were a single thing: an explosion, a blur, a result. But the athletes inside it experience something stranger — a race with chapters, each one only a handful of strides long.

## The block phase

The first thing to go is patience. The gun rewards a body that has learned to *fall* before it pushes, to trust the angle and not stand up too soon. Stand up early and the race is already lost; you just won't feel it for another fifty metres.

## Drive and transition

For the first thirty metres the runner is barely upright, shins close to the track, each step landing behind the hips. Then comes the most delicate moment of the whole race — the transition, where the body rises without slowing. Get it wrong and you brake. Get it right and nothing visible happens at all.

## Top speed, and holding on

Nobody accelerates through the line. The race is won by whoever *decelerates least*. The final twenty metres are an exercise in staying relaxed while everything in the body wants to clench.

Ten breaths, give or take. That is the whole event. Everything else — the years, the mornings, the small adjustments — is compressed into a window shorter than a yawn.
